Circulatory System

Mammals have a heart divided into 4 chambers and its made of 2 atria and 2 ventricles. The right atrium receives blood from the superior and inferior vena cavas and the coronary sinus. After, blood moves to the right ventricle and is then pumped to the lungs. The lungs put oxygen back into the lungs and sends it to the left atrium where it then moves to the left ventricle using the bicuspid valve. The blood then moves out of the left ventricle to the aorta which sends the blood around the blood. Mammals have more complex circulatory systems than other organisms.

All mammals reproduce sexually. While all mammals give birth to live young, monotremes lay eggs. Mammals have different matting systems such as monogamy and polygamy. Monogamy is where one of each sex have and exclusive relationship. Polygamy is when one of the sex will have more relationships. Mammals have mammary glands which they use to produce milk for young. Mammals can be placental or marsupial. Placental mammals have a placenta which is an opening for exchanging nutrients and wastes between the mother and the fetus. Marsupials are mammals commonly found in Australia that give birth to weak young that remains in their pouch. It stays in the pouch for some time. There are mammary glands located there to nourish the baby. Monotremes are mammals that lay eggs with not hard, but leathery shells.

Nervous System

Mammalian brains are much more complex, allowing them a variety of responses. Mammals have better cerebral hemispheres allowing them more responses to stimuli. Members in this class have the ability to learn, adapt to different scenarios and behaviors not seen in other animals. Many animals have similar behaviors and physiological functions. The nervous system is split into the central and peripheral nervous systems. The peripheral nervous system control volitional and nonvolitional behaviors using cranial and spinal nerves. The central nervous system is made of the forebrain, midbrain and hindbrain with each unit performing a variety of tasks. It is difficult to sum up mammalian nervous systems as many of them have very different nervous systems due to their varying levels of senses.

Excretory System

Mammals have kidneys made of units called nephrons. Each nephron has a portion called the loop of Henle or nephritic loop. These loops give the ability to make urine with higher concentration of solutes (even higher than that of the blood). Mammals don't have a renal portal system. This is a system of veins that moves blood from the posterior end of the mammal to the kidneys. Every vertebrate (except Jawless Fish) have this. Every mammal has a urinary bladder which stores urine.

Digestive System

Mammals possess a complete digestive tract. Digestive tracts vary based on what the animal eats but for the most part, they are very similar. Herbivores possess elongated intestines and specialized stomachs. The stomach can also have subdivisions based on where the animal lives and what it eats. For example, some animals may have subdivisions for different kinds of symbiotic bacteria which help the animal break down otherwise indigestible compounds.
